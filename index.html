Australia's Digital Divide

Australia is one of the most digitally connected nations in the world, yet access to technology and high-quality internet is still unequal. A digital inclusion score measures how well people can access, afford and use digital technologies. Higher scores suggest stronger digital participation, while lower scores reveal communities that face barriers to online education, employment, healthcare and communication.

01

Australia is highly connected overall

Australia has a strong national digital inclusion score, but the national average hides clear differences between states, regions and social groups. This story explores where the divide exists, why it happens and who is most affected.

02

Digital inclusion differs across Australia

The map shows that digital inclusion is not evenly distributed. Metropolitan and higher-income states generally perform better, while regional-heavy areas face greater barriers.

03

States can be directly compared

Comparing states side-by-side makes the gap clearer. The ACT has the strongest score, while Tasmania records the lowest, showing that digital participation varies across the country.

04

Income strongly influences digital inclusion

Higher-income households are more likely to afford reliable internet, newer devices and digital education. This creates a strong connection between wealth and digital participation.

05

Income inequality exists between states

Median household income varies significantly across Australia. Since affordability is a major part of digital inclusion, economic inequality directly affects who can participate online.

06

Digital inclusion has improved over time

Australia has experienced steady improvements in digital inclusion over time due to expanding internet access, smartphone adoption, online education and digital government services. The graph also includes an estimated global benchmark, showing that Australia continues to perform above broader international participation levels.

07

The NBN accelerated internet access

The National Broadband Network significantly improved internet access across Australia, but connection quality still differs between technologies. Fibre connections generally provide faster and more reliable performance, while fixed wireless services often experience lower speeds and stability in regional communities.

08

Some communities are further behind

The digital divide is not only geographic. Low-income households, older Australians, First Nations Australians and people with disability continue to record lower digital inclusion scores. This highlights that affordability, accessibility and digital skills remain major barriers for disadvantaged communities.

09

Regional Australia faces additional barriers

Regional and remote communities often face weaker infrastructure, lower service quality and affordability pressures compared to metropolitan areas. Greater reliance on wireless technologies and reduced access to high-speed fibre networks contribute to lower digital participation outside Australia's largest cities.

10

The divide is made up of different weaknesses

Digital inclusion depends on access, affordability and digital ability. This heatmap shows that states can perform well in one area while still having weaknesses in another.

11

Digital inclusion has multiple components

Access alone is not enough. People also need affordable connections and the skills required to use digital services confidently and safely.

12

Some groups remain digitally excluded

Despite national improvements, disadvantaged groups still experience lower digital participation. This shows that infrastructure progress alone does not solve the digital divide.

13

Connection technology also matters

Different internet technologies provide different performance levels. Fibre connections generally offer faster and more reliable internet than older copper-based systems.

Key Insight
Fibre connections provide substantially higher speeds than fixed wireless and satellite technologies, helping reduce digital exclusion in connected areas.
14

Digital inclusion is about more than internet access

Australia's digital landscape has improved significantly, but the benefits are not shared equally. Closing the digital divide requires continued investment in affordable internet, regional infrastructure, education and digital skills so all Australians can participate fully in modern society.

15

Key Takeaways

Income remains one of the strongest predictors of digital inclusion. States and communities with higher household incomes consistently achieve better digital participation outcomes, highlighting the importance of affordability in accessing digital services.

Geography continues to shape opportunity. Regional and remote Australians face additional challenges due to infrastructure limitations, service quality differences and reduced access to high-speed broadband technologies.

The NBN has significantly improved national connectivity. Broadband access has expanded across Australia, but differences in connection technologies mean internet quality and reliability still vary between communities.

Vulnerable groups remain at greater risk of exclusion. Low-income households, older Australians, First Nations Australians and people with disability continue to record lower digital inclusion scores than the national average.

Digital inclusion requires more than internet access. Meaningful participation in modern society depends on affordable services, digital skills and reliable infrastructure working together to ensure all Australians can benefit from the digital economy.
